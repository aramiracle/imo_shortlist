Algebra

8 problems Functional equations · Inequalities · Polynomials

A1
Easy
Let $a, b, c$ be positive real numbers. Prove that $$32 \!(\sum_{\mathrm{cyc}} ab(a+b))^{\!3} \;\ge\; 27 \!(\prod_{\mathrm{cyc}}(a+b))^{\!2} \cdot(\prod_{\mathrm{cyc}}(a+b) - 4abc).$$
A2
Easy
Prove that there do not exist rational numbers $a$ and $b$ such that $$\sqrt[3]{2} \;=\; \sqrt{a} + \sqrt{b}.$$
A3
Medium
Let $n \ge 3$ be an integer, and let $x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n$ be real numbers satisfying: $$\sum_{i=1}^n x_i = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad \sum_{i=1}^n x_i^2 = n(n-1).$$ Prove that: $$\sum_{i=1}^n x_i^3 \le n(n-1)(n-2).$$ Determine all configurations of $(x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n)$ for which equality holds.
A4
Hard
Let $a, b, c$ be non-negative real numbers such that $ab + bc + ca = 1$. Prove that: $$\sqrt{a^3+b+c} + \sqrt{b^3+c+a} + \sqrt{c^3+a+b} \;\ge\; \sqrt{(a+b+c)^3 + 4(a+b+c)^2 - (a+b+c) - 4}$$
A5
Hard
Find all surjective functions $f\colon \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ satisfying $$f(x f(y)) + f(y f(x)) = f(x)f(y) + f(xy)$$ for all $x, y \in \mathbb{R}$.
A6
Hard
Determine all polynomials $P(x)$ with real coefficients for which there exists a rational function $Q(x)$ with real coefficients, not identically zero, such that $$P(x) \;=\; \frac{Q(x)}{Q\!\left(1 - \dfrac{1}{x}\right)}$$ for all real numbers $x$ except for a finite number of values where the expressions are undefined.

Note: A rational function is an expression of the form $A(x)/B(x)$, where $A(x)$ and $B(x)$ are polynomials with real coefficients and $B(x)$ is not the zero polynomial.
A7
Hard
Let $a_1, a_2, a_3, \dots$ be a sequence of positive real numbers satisfying $a_1 = 1$ and $$a_{n+1} = a_n + \frac{n}{a_1 + a_2 + \dots + a_n}$$ for all integers $n \ge 1$.

Prove that for every positive integer $n$, $$a_n \ge \sqrt{\frac{6n^3 - 3n^2 - 3n + 2}{2n^2}}.$$
A8
Challenging
Find all functions $f\colon \mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{N}$ satisfying $$f(abc) + f\!\left(2a\,f(b)\right) + f\!\left(2b\,f(c)\right) + f\!\left(2c\,f(a)\right) = f(a)\,f(b)\,f(c)$$ for all $a, b, c \in \mathbb{N}$.

Combinatorics

8 problems Game theory · Graph theory · Sequences · Number sets

C1
Easy
Maryam and Iman play a game on a $7\times 7$ chessboard. Initially the board is empty.
  1. On the first turn, Maryam places a piece on any square of her choice.
  2. In subsequent turns, each player must move the piece to an adjacent square (sharing a common edge) not previously visited.
  3. The player who cannot make a valid move loses.

Prove that Maryam (the first player) has a winning strategy, and describe it in detail.
C2
Medium
There are $n$ students at a university. Some students form several clubs, and these clubs are grouped into $s$ societies. The following conditions are satisfied:
  1. No two clubs share more than one student.
  2. For each student $u$ and each society $S$, the student $u$ belongs to exactly two clubs of $S$.
  3. For any society $S$, any two of its clubs share exactly one student.

Prove that the total number of pairs of distinct clubs $\{C_a, C_b\}$ that belong to the same society is exactly $n \cdot s$.
C3
Medium
For any integer $n \ge 2$, prove that there exists a set $S$ consisting of $2n$ distinct triangular numbers that can be partitioned into two subsets of size $n$ with equal sums.

A triangular number is a positive integer of the form $\tfrac{k(k+1)}{2}$ for some positive integer $k$.
C4
Hard
In a country there is a one-way route between every pair of cities, and each route is exclusively designated for one of three modes of transportation: car, train, or airplane. Prove that one can choose a set of three cities such that every other city in the country can reach at least one of these three cities using only a single mode of transportation.
C5
Hard
A coin is placed on each vertex of a finite simple graph $G$. In each step, we may select a vertex $v$ containing a coin, collect its coin, and discard the coins on $v$ and all of its neighbors. This process is repeated until no coins remain on the graph.

Prove that we can always collect at least $$\sum_{v \in V} \frac{1}{\deg(v) + 1}$$ coins.
C6
Challenging
In a high council, there are $m$ sages and $n$ secret truths, where $n \ge m \ge 2$. Each sage knows a unique combination of these $n$ truths (meaning that for any two sages, there is at least one truth known by one sage and not by the other).

The High Priest decides to select a single truth to seal away forever. A truth is called safe if, after it is sealed, all $m$ sages can still be uniquely identified by the combinations of remaining truths they know.

Prove that there are at least $n - m + 1$ different truths that are individually safe to seal.
C7
Challenging
At the beginning of a game, there are $n$ piles, each containing a single token of value $1$. In each step, we are allowed to choose any two piles, say with values $A$ and $B$, and merge them into a single pile. The value of this new pile is defined to be: $$A + B + \min(A, B)$$ This process is repeated $n-1$ times until only a single pile remains.

Prove that the maximum possible value of the final pile is equal to the number of odd entries in the first $n$ rows of Pascal's triangle (consisting of the binomial coefficients $\binom{y}{x}$ where $0 \le x \le y < n$).
C8
Challenging
Let $m \ge 3$ be an odd integer. A school has $m$ students and $n$ clubs, where $n \ge m + 2$. Each student can be a member of any number of clubs (including none), but no two clubs have the exact same set of members.

For any two distinct clubs, their discord is defined as the number of students who belong to exactly one of them. Let $d_{\min}$ and $d_{\max}$ be the minimum and maximum discords among all pairs of clubs, respectively.

Prove that: $$\frac{d_{\max}}{d_{\min}} \ge \frac{m+3}{m-1}$$ and show that this bound is tight for all odd integers $m \ge 3$.

Geometry

8 problems Euclidean · Projective · Circle geometry · Concyclicity

G1
Easy
Let a lune be the region bounded between two internally tangent circles. Given $N$ points in the plane, prove that for any non-negative integers $P$, $Q$, $R$ with $P + Q + R = N$, there exists a lune containing exactly $P$ points strictly inside the smaller circle, $Q$ points in the strict interior of the lune (the annular region), and $R$ points strictly outside the larger circle.
G2
Medium
Let the incircle of triangle $ABC$ with incenter $I$ touch sides $BC$, $CA$, $AB$ at $D$, $E$, $F$ respectively. Let $M$ be the intersection of lines $AB$ and $DE$. The line through $M$ perpendicular to $IM$ meets lines $DF$ and $EF$ at $P$ and $Q$ respectively. Prove that $MP = MQ$.
G3
Medium
Let $ABCD$ be a cyclic quadrilateral inscribed in a circle with center $O$. Let $AB$ and $CD$ meet at $E$, and let $AD$ and $BC$ meet at $F$. Let the diagonals $AC$ and $BD$ meet at $M$, and let $N$ be the reflection of $M$ across line $EF$. Prove that $N$, $E$, $O$, $F$ are concyclic.
G4
Hard
Let $ABC$ be a triangle with $AB < AC$, and let its incircle touch sides $CA$ and $AB$ at $E$ and $F$, respectively. The line $EF$ intersects line $BC$ at $L$. Let the incircle of triangle $LEC$ and the $L$-excircle of triangle $LFB$ touch $EF$ at $N$ and $M$, respectively. Prove that $BM$, $CN$, and the bisector of $\angle A$ are concurrent on the incircle of $\triangle ABC$.
G5
Hard
Let $ABC$ be a scalene triangle with incenter $I$. The incircle of $\triangle ABC$ touches side $BC$ at $D$. Let $AD$ intersect the incircle again at $E$. Let $P$ and $Q$ be the intersections of the internal and external angle bisectors of $\angle A$ with line $BC$, respectively, and let the circumcircle of $\triangle APQ$ and the median $AM$ intersect again at $N$. If $F$ lies on segment $AD$ such that $AE = DF$, prove that $A$, $F$, $I$, $N$ are concyclic.
G6
Challenging
Let $P$ be a set of $n \ge 6$ points on a circle such that no three of their connecting chords are concurrent in the interior. Let $S$ be the set of all interior intersection points of these chords. Find, in terms of $n$, the exact number of lines containing at least three points of $S$ but no point of $P$.
G7
Challenging
Let $ABC$ be a scalene triangle, and let its $A$-excircle touch side $BC$ at $D$. Let $M$ be the midpoint of the altitude from $A$. The line $MD$ intersects the excircle again at $T$. Let $S$ be a point on line $MD$ such that $B$, $S$, $C$, $T$ are concyclic. Prove that $S$ lies on the perpendicular bisector of $BC$.
G8
Challenging
Let $\angle xAy$ be a given angle with vertex $A$ in the plane. Let $\Gamma$ be a given circular arc belonging to a circle $\omega$. The center $P$ of the circle is not marked, and no other tools, lengths, or points are given. The arc $\Gamma$ is simply a standalone curved segment in the plane.

Using only an unmarked straightedge, construct the angle bisector of $\angle xAy$.

Provide a step-by-step description of your construction, and prove that it is mathematically correct.

Number Theory

8 problems Diophantine equations · Arithmetic functions · Sequences

N1
Easy
Find all pairs of positive integers $(x, y)$ satisfying the equation $$x^y - y^x = x + y.$$
N2
Medium
Find all quadruples of positive integers $(a, b, c, d)$ such that $$a^2 + b^2 + c^2 + d^2 \;\mid\; (a + b + c + d)^2.$$
N3
Hard
Let $G$ be the infinite graph with vertex set $\mathbb{Z}_{>0}$, where two distinct vertices $a$ and $b$ are adjacent if and only if $$\frac{\operatorname{lcm}(a,b)}{\gcd(a,b)} > a + b.$$ For any integer $n > 2$, let $G_n$ be the subgraph of $G$ induced on $\{1,2,\dots,n\}$. Prove that the clique number of $G_n$ equals exactly the number of primes at most $n$.
N4
Hard
Determine all positive integers $m$ with the following property: there exists a positive integer $n$ such that $$x^2 + my^2 = n$$ is solvable in rationals, but for every positive integer $k < m$ the equation $$x^2 + ky^2 = n$$ is not solvable in rationals.
N5
Hard
Let $n \ge 3$ be an integer. A sequence of positive integers $a_1, a_2, \dots, a_n$ satisfies the recurrence relation $$a_k = \frac{[a_{k-1}, a_{k-2}]}{(a_{k-1}, a_{k-2})} \quad \text{for all } 3 \le k \le n$$ where $[x, y]$ and $(x, y)$ denote the least common multiple and greatest common divisor of $x$ and $y$, respectively.

Prove that if the sequence is strictly decreasing ($a_1 > a_2 > \dots > a_n$), then the first term $a_1$ satisfies the inequality $$a_1 \ge \frac{2^{n-2} a_{n-1}}{a_n}.$$
N6
Hard
Let $\mathbb{N}$ denote the set of positive integers. Let $\mathcal{F}$ be the set of all bijective functions $f\colon \mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{N}$ satisfying $f(ab) = f(a)f(b)$ for all $a, b \in \mathbb{N}$.

For each positive integer $n$, we define $g(n) = \min_{f \in \mathcal{F}} f(n)$.

Prove that $g(g(n)) = g(n)$ for all positive integers $n$.
N7
Challenging
A sequence of positive integers $(a_n)$ is defined by $$a_1 = 2,\quad a_2 = 4,\quad a_{n+2} = \binom{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\quad\text{for all }n \ge 1.$$ Prove that for every prime $p$, there exists a positive integer $n$ such that $p \mid a_n$.
N8
Challenging
A lattice point $P$ in the plane is called visible from the origin $O(0,0)$ if the line segment $OP$ contains no other lattice points except $O$ and $P$.

Prove that for every positive integer $k$, there exists a $(2k+1) \times (2k+1)$ square of lattice points in the plane whose center point is visible from the origin, while all other $(2k+1)^2 - 1$ lattice points in the square are not visible from the origin.
